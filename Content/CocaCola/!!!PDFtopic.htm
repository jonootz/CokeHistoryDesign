Lettering

The base logo uses a script type font called "" which was a popular style in the late 1900's. This was the original logo except it is more refined now whereas originally it was handwritten. The Dynamic Wave

The "dynamic ribbon" is another element that usually is placed below the lettering. It is called many things such as the swirl, the spiral, the wave, and the ribbon. The color of the wave matches the color of the lettering. It basically starts to the left of the lettering and curves downward below the "Cola" part where it becomes much thinner, then it curves upward and thickens as it goes to the right of the lettering. This element is so recogniable that with the "Share a Coke" campaign, the wave can be used with ideas and names in place of the "Coca-Cola" script.

Colors

The colors are red and white. The basic logo is the red lettering with a transparent or white background. But it is more commonly used on packaging and advertising inverted, with the red for the background and white is used for the lettering depending on where and how it is used.

The Bottle

The bottle design is an alternative logo that is widely used as a graphic because of how recognizable it is. The "contour" bottle, as it is commonly known, reflects the combination of both. The shape of the bottle represents the coca leaf. The base is wide, then it curves in then out as you reach the middle part. The middle is wide for approximately a third of the bottles height so that it can be held easily then it curves inward towards the bottle opening where it is capped. The logo label or embossed logo goes in the middle area. The texture of the bottle represents the kola bean. There are raised vertical ridges that appear around the bottle that represent the kola bean. The label with the logo goes across the middles section which is flat and free of the ridges. Who was the designer(s)?

The logo

The logo was designed and penned by 's bookkeeper Frank Mason Robinson in 1885. Robinson suggested the name "Coca-Cola" after the two main ingredients. Cocaine which was taken from the coca leaves and caffeine taken from kola nuts. He suggested to Pemberton that instead of a "K" that they use a "C" because the two "C"s would appear more catchy in advertising (Boney, 1987). In regards to the cocaine, started using spent leaves insteas of fresh ones after 1904 because the only had trace levels of cocaine left in them yet still provided the necassary taste. Since then uses cocaine free coca leaf extract prepared at a Stephan Company in Maywood New Jersey. After 1904, instead of using fresh leaves, Coca-Cola started using "spent" leaves – the leftovers of the cocaine-extraction process with trace levels of cocaine. Since then, Coca-Cola uses a cocaine-free coca leaf extract prepared at a Stepan Company plant in Maywood, New Jersey (May, 1988.

The bottle

Benjamin Thomas, co-founder of the Coca-Cola Bottling Company, came up with a solution to the problem was facing at the time regarding other bottling companies were trying to copy-cat Coke. It is said that he stated, “We need a bottle which a person can recognize as a Coca-Cola bottle when he feels it in the dark.” The Coca-Cola Company decided to launch a competition among bottle suppliers. The objective was to come up with a distinctive design (1915 Coca-Cola Prototype Bottle and Concept Sketch). Earl R. Dean who worked for the Root Glass Bottling company came up with the design basing it on the same two ingredients that inspired Robinson for the name. But Dean was inspired by the shapes of the kola nut and the coca leaf.

 

The Wave

In 1969 the "Arden Square with the Dynamic Ribbon Device (commonly called the "wave") was introduced" (The Company, 2012).

The Disc

Back in 1947 designed a Red Disc, also known as the “button” sign and has been used from time to time since 1947 which may have been inspired by the circular signs from the 1930's. "In 1948, the discs began to be hung inside a place of business as advertising and decoration". The Red Disc appeared in print advertising up through the 1960’s when the Arciform or “fish tail” signs began to be used. (Fletcher, 2014). There have been many variations of it such as just the white logo, then they added the contour bottle illutration behind it, then in the 1990's they re-used it with the slogan "Always" above it in a green (sometimes blue) banner that followed the curve of the circle on top. In the 2000's it was made flat with no metallic shine and displayed the main logo with the wave in it. Most recently they dropped the wave and brought back a subtle shine.

The first version of the Coca-Cola logo was in capital serif letters in the first newspaper advertisement for the drink which appeared in the Atlanta Journal on May 29th, 1886 (Martin).

The original version of the was reportedly first used in June 16, 1887 but it took over 5 years to register it in 1892 then the trademark registration was granted on January 31, 1893. Until this point the logo was always hand written so it was not always consistent in design in regards to the flow and thickness of the letters, the slant may have been a little off, the tail to the left of the first "o" was not always drawn in, which was dropped anyway in the more modern versions (Martin). The logo used red right from the start. in it advertising.

Before the version was granted its first trademark from the patent office in 1890-1891 fancy serif font was also used. The "C"s both had tails on the bottom and the tops of them swirled into itself like a spiral. Below the lowest apart of the spiral on the inside two swirls dangled from it (The Logo Story).

There was another variation with diamonds floating inside the "C"s instead of the dangling swirls in 1892.

But once the version was trademarked that is all that was used and the word trademark would appear on the tail of the first "C" underneath.

In 1903 the label "trademark registered" began to appear in the swooping tail of the letter "C" because a new law required companies to re-register their trademarks ( The History of ). The colors red and white were prominent in the adverts and green was an accent color use quite frequently. The ads consisted of both graphic design that focused on just typography and others where the center piece was either the contour bottle (after that came around), grpahics of soda fountains, and sophisticated women holding the glasses or bottles of Coke.

bottlers wanted to come up with a solution to a major problem at the time. By the 1940s, the word Trademark was removed from the tail and placed underneath the logo and the script had evolved to its most familiar design in which the script was slightly altered to be even and more circular meaning that each letter had the perfect slant and the form of each letter is more rounded.
