Coca-Cola is a carbonated soft drink that is produced by The Coca-Cola Company of Atlanta, Georgia. In 1886 the drink was founded by who was a pharmacist trying to create an elixir for medicinal purposes. The syrup was to be mixed with seltzer water from soda fountains, which were very popular back in the late 1900's. "The first servings of Coca‑Cola were sold for 5 cents per glass. During the first year, sales averaged a modest nine servings per day in Atlanta. Today, daily servings of Coca‑Cola beverages are estimated at 1.9 billion globally" (Coca-Cola History).

The logo was designed and penned by 's bookkeeper Frank Mason Robinson in 1885. Robinson suggested the name "Coca-Cola" after the two main ingredients. Cocaine which was taken from the coca leaves and caffeine taken from kola nuts. He suggested to Pemberton that instead of a "K" that they use a "C" because the two "C"s would appear more catchy in advertising (Boney, 1987).

So Robinson penned the script himself using Spencerian Style script which was very popular at the time, the script was even used in schools when teaching children how to write.

In 1892, the Coca-Cola formula and brand were acquired by a business man named . His marketing tactics led the distribution of Coca‑Cola to expand to soda fountains beyond Atlanta, then to its dominance of the world soft-drink market throughout the 20th century (sureewoong).

In 1894 Mississippi a shop owner named Joseph A. Biedenharn was impressed by Coca‑Cola that he bottled it in what was a common glass bottle at the time called a "Hutchinson" and sent a case to Asa Griggs Candler. The Company started with one drink, in 1886 and over the years they have evolved into a corporation that owns thousands of drinks and branding. Even though the Company itself has it's own logo I want to focus on it's flagship product . The company logo "The Company" is a great example of how recognizable and unique the branding of is - they simply insert the words "The" before the logo and "Company" after it in the same . There are various versions that are currently being used that make up 's corporate identity system. The main logo is "Coca-Cola" written in which is now a modernized version of the original script logo scripted by Robinson. Over the course of it's history it has evolved, and currently they use four elements (in addition to color) that were introduced over time in different ways, both interchangeably and separately. The main components of the official logo are made of five elements: The lettering, the colors, the "dynamic wave", the bottle shape, and of course the "Coke" nickname.

The base logo uses a script type font called "" which was a popular style in the late 1900's. This was the original logo except it is more refined now whereas originally it was handwritten. The script spells out the words "Coca" and "Cola" with a hyphen in the middle. The first "C" has a tail on the bottom that underlines the "oca". The second "C" has a tail on top that swoops over the top of "ola" and it threads through the "l". Currently this can be use with and with any of the other elements and is at the forefront of their corporate website. The colors are red and white. The basic logo is the red lettering with a transparent or white background. But it is more commonly used on packaging and advertising as inverted, with the red for the background and white is used for the lettering depending on where and how it is used.

In addition to the main logo, Coca-Cola uses a few more designs to represent the brand and they are very important to the corporate identity.

The "dynamic ribbon" or "dynamic wave" is another element that usually is placed below the lettering. It is called many things such as the swirl, the spiral, the wave, and the ribbon. The color of the wave matches the color of the lettering. It basically starts to the left of the lettering and curves downward below the word "Cola" where it becomes much thinner, then it curves upward and thickens as it goes to the right of the lettering. This element is so recognizable that with the recent "Share a Coke" campaign, the wave can be used with ideas or names in place of the "Coca-Cola" script. In 1969 the "Arden Square with the Dynamic Ribbon Device (commonly called the "wave") was introduced" (The Company, 2012).

The bottle design is an alternative logo that is widely used as a graphic because of how recognizable it is. The bottle itself was originally designed with the soda's two main ingredients in mind, the coca leaf and the kola nut. The coca leaf is narrow at the stem and widens in the middle and curves to a point. The kola bean typically has ridged shell which causes lines that go from end to end. The "contour" bottle, as it is commonly known, reflects the combination of both. The shape of the bottle represents the coca leaf. The base is wide, then it curves in, then widens as you reach the middle part. The middle is wide for approximately a third of the bottles height so that it can be held easily, then it curves inward towards the bottle opening where it is capped. The logo label or embossed logo goes in the middle area. The texture of the bottle represents the kola bean. There are raised vertical ridges that appear around the bottle that represent the kola bean. The label goes across the middles section which is flat and free of the ridges. The logo can be a label, or white lettering, or embossed lettering, and all three versions use the with and without the wave. The bottle has been used on the first can to help people recognize that Coke was now in a can. The in the 1990's it was used again on cans with the "Always" campaign. Starting back in 2009, the bottle shape has been used frequently used as a graphic with the silhouette of the bottle design colored red or white and the stretched across the middle.

The disc is also an alternative logo. It is just a circle with a flat red color and the white inside of it. The latest version does have a slight shine on the left edge and a slight shadow on the right edge. Back in 1947 designed a Red Disc, also known as the “button” sign and has been used from time to time since 1947 which may have been inspired by the circular signs from the 1930's. "In 1948, the discs began to be hung inside a place of business as advertising and decoration". The Red Disc appeared in print advertising up through the 1960’s when the Arciform or “fish tail” signs began to be used. (Fletcher, 2014). There have been many variations of it such as just the white logo, then they added the contour bottle illutration behind it, then in the 1990's they re-used it with the slogan "Always" above it in a green (sometimes blue) banner that followed the curve of the circle on top. In the 2000's it was made flat with no metallic shine and displayed the main logo with the wave in it. Most recently they dropped the wave and brought back a subtle shine.

And finally in 1966 started using another alternative logo, one to represent the nickname for the drink that is most commonly used, "Coke". This uses a serif font and spells "Coke".

 

Benjamin Thomas, co-founder of the Coca-Cola Bottling Company, came up with a solution to the problem was facing at the time regarding other bottling companies were trying to copy-cat Coke. It is said that he stated, “We need a bottle which a person can recognize as a Coca-Cola bottle when he feels it in the dark.” The Coca-Cola Company decided to launch a competition among bottle suppliers. The objective was to come up with a distinctive design (1915 Coca-Cola Prototype Bottle and Concept Sketch). Earl R. Dean who worked for the Root Glass Bottling company came up with the design basing it on the same two ingredients that inspired Robinson for the name. But Dean was inspired by the shapes of the kola nut and the coca leaf.

Regarding the history and developement, the first version of the Coca-Cola logo was in capital serif letters in the first newspaper advertisement for the drink which appeared in the Atlanta Journal on May 29th, 1886 (Martin). The original version of the was reportedly first used in June 16, 1887 but it took over 5 years to register it in 1892 then the trademark registration was granted on January 31, 1893. Until this point the logo was always hand written so it was not always consistent in design in regards to the flow and thickness of the letters, the slant may have been a little off, the tail to the left of the first "o" was not always drawn in, which was dropped anyway in the more modern versions (Martin). The logo used red right from the start. in it advertising.

However just before the version was granted its first trademark from the patent office, in 1890-1891 a fancy serif font version was also used sometimes. The "C"s both had tails on the bottom and the tops of them swirled into itself like a spiral. Below the lowest part of the spiral on the inside there were two swirls that dangled from it (The Logo Story). There was another variation with diamonds floating inside the "C"s instead of the dangling swirls in 1892.

But once the version was trademarked that is all that was used and the word trademark would appear on the tail of the first "C" underneath. In 1903 the label "trademark registered" began to appear in the swooping tail of the letter "C" because a new law required companies to re-register their trademarks ( The History of ). The colors red and white were prominent in the adverts while green was an accent color used quite frequently. The ads consisted of both graphic design that focused on just typography and others where the center piece was either the contour bottle (after that came around), or graphics of soda fountains, as well as sophisticated looking women holding the glasses or bottles of Coke.

bottlers wanted to come up with a solution to a major problem at the time. Many 'copycat' bottlers were trying to take advantage of the name by naming their drinks in a similar way and using the same straight edge bottles was known for. So approached many glass manufacturers to come up with a unique bottle design for them. The Root Glass Company of Terre Haute, Indiana, was the winner of the contest as Earl R.Dean (who worked for Root Glass) designed the famous contour shaped bottle. Coca‑Cola approved it in 1915 and it was introduced to the consumer in 1916 (Hobbs).

By the 1920's, the lettering and symmetry was closer to what the modern version looks like except for the required TRADE MARK REGISTERED lettering in the tail. In the mid 1920s the word "DRINK" would also appear above the logo in much of their signage and advertising, along with the infamous "5 cents per glass" pricing (The History of ).

By the 1940s, the word Trademark was removed from the tail and placed underneath the logo and the script had evolved to its most familiar design in which the script was slightly altered to be even and more circular, meaning that each letter had the perfect slant and the form of each letter is more rounded. This is the form that we still see today. Also in 1941 the public saw the first use of the nickname "Coke" as an official trademark (Logopedia). From this point on, this will be the base logo and in years to come, elements will be added to and there are times when this logo, just the will be used alone. Up until 1957 the words were blown into the glass, but in 1957 it was printed with white label on glass and trademarked (100 years of the Coca-Cola Bottle). The fishtail design was introduce d in 1958 and lasted until 1969. The script was placed in a red Arciform shape and on each end there where triangular cutouts which made it look like fish tails.

The "famous" diamond can was the first introduced in 1960. The body was red with a white diamond as the center piece outlined in silver. The Script logo was inside of the diamond and behind that was a line drawing of the contour bottle so that people would recognize that it was in a can (http://www.coca-colacompany.com/timeline-the-evolution-of-the-coca-cola-bottle/#1960). In 1966 the Coke can received it's first overhaul and is commonly known as the "Harlequin design", that is sometimes indicated as the small diamond can (Coke Can History).

The logo for "Coke" started appearing in on the aluminum cans too, and one of the first slogans to be used with the new graphic was the "Things Go Better With Coke" campaign in 1965.

In 1969 introduced the "Arden Square" as their brand new logo. It featured the classic in white with the famous 'Dynamic Ribbon, or 'White Swirl' for the first time. Above the script in sans-serif was the word "Enjoy". They were laid over a red square. This logo lasted for almost 20 years until Coke was re-branded in 1987 (The History of )

In 1971 a song from a Coca-Cola commercial called "I'd Like to Teach the World to Sing", produced by Billy Davis, became a hit single and was featured in the famous commercial dubbed I would like to buy the world a coke. In 1977 the bottle itself was granted registration as a trademark. A earlier study showed that only less than 1% of Americans could not identify the bottle by shape alone (http://www.coca-colacompany.com/timeline-the-evolution-of-the-coca-cola-bottle/#1977).

The cola wars occured in the 1980's, and "For the first time in its history, Coca Cola was no longer the most popular soft drink. By this point Pepsi's popularity and sales had surpassed their own and the company was in full damage control mode. In response the company changed its formula and launched a bold new brand, snubbing its script logo in favor of a thick slab serif font to accompany their rebrand as Coke(http://fineprintnyc.com/blog/the-history-of-the-coca-cola-logo). Enter "New Coke" which was 's solution to beat out Pepsi. The company changed the serif version of "Coke" instead of the traditional logo. They also added silver to outline the word "Coke" and added another layer to the wave which was silver. In addition to that they slapped a silver label on the top of the can with the word "New" in it. Despite all their efforts and regardless if the drink tasted good, many long time Coke drinkers and fans created such a backlash that was forced to bring back the original formula. Enter "Coca-Cola Classic". "Classic" was added to the name to differentiate the two sodas and New Coke was renamed to Coke II. But required redesigns. Coke II took on a slanted serif font and both it and the wave were accented with blue outlines. Then Coke Classic updated the traditional can look with the words going up sideways on the can, but instead of the wave, then used the word "Classic" along with a new icon indicating "Original Formula". By the 1990's the look was updated again whereas the ribbon graphic was integrated further into the script itself, intersecting with the "o" in Cola(http://fineprintnyc.com/blog/the-history-of-the-coca-cola-logo). By 1993, with the "Always" campaign, the wave is gone again and focused on bringing the traditional graphics such as the disc, the contour bottle and bottle caps onto the can.

In 1993 a 20 oz PET bottle is created to help distinguish the plastic bottles from other soft drink bottles just like the glass version did so many years before by making a "Contour" version.(http://www.coca-colacompany.com/timeline-the-evolution-of-the-coca-cola-bottle/#1993)

In the 2000's the Dynamic Ribbon, (the wave) comes back but with bubbles, and yellow outlines, and thicker with more lines in it. "Coca-Cola Classic" name was reverted back to "Coca-Cola" since there was no longer any "New Coke" production. The disc becomes simplified "minimal version of the classic red disc was reintroduced for corporate / retail purposes"(http://fineprintnyc.com/blog/the-history-of-the-coca-cola-logo). Then the wave becomes simpler like it was before New Coke era - The can retained the red coloring and the white lettering with a simplified swirl.

Open happiness campaign is an homage to an earlier campaign “The Pause That Refreshes” from 1929 inviting people all around to world to relax with the refreshment of a coke (https://www.worldofcoca-cola.com/about-us/coca-cola-history/)

"One Brand" is a refresh for 's branding. They are combining all related "Coke" products under the umbrella as far as branding goes. For instance the original formula (the flagship product) is now displayed horizontally across the can like it has always has been on the bottles. The traditional logo script spans across the can once taking up the top half of the can and below it is the white dynamic wave. The other three main "Coke" related products: Life, Zero, and Diet Coke follow the same design, all now just display on the upper half of the can just like the flagship except Diet Coke which displays Diet Coke but now in the same to match better. Each also have the wave placed the same way. This really ties the four products together. Each can does have a unique color and accent color that they retain from previous designs. The Life's can is green and the accent color, that includes the wave color and font color are both white just like regular Coke's. The Zero's can is black with accent color being red, and Diet Coke in which the can is silver and red as the accent color with the exception of "Diet" which has been black for many years now. Below the main logo and above the wave in much smaller font are descriptions that identify the differences of each product and also displayed is the unique names: Life, zero which appear as subtitles in a way which is a powerful way to signify that they are really part of the family.
